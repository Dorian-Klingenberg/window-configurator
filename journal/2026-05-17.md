# Journal — May 17, 2026

## Stained Glass Insert Product Concept

Explored a product extension idea today: **parametric stained glass inserts as a new line item type within the existing WindowConfigurator platform.**

---

## The Core Idea

A configurator for stained glass inserts — decorative leaded glass panels, custom-dimensioned to fit any window opening — built on the same session/tenant/webhook architecture already designed for window configuration.

The pattern library stores designs as relative coordinate geometry (0.0–1.0 space). A scaling/tiling engine maps patterns to the actual daylight opening. The homeowner picks a pattern, enters their measurement, and sees exactly what it looks like. The fabricator receives a precise cut list and frame spec. The design consultation problem disappears.

---

## The Manufacturing Model

The insert consists of:

1. A **leaded glass field** fabricated by a stained glass studio from the configurator's cut list output
2. A **solid metal border** (aluminum or zinc channel) that the field terminates inside — never touching the edge — providing a flat, continuous, gaskettable perimeter
3. An **IGU sealed unit** — the bordered panel sandwiched between two clear panes by a glass fabricator

The metal border is the key structural insight. It separates the artisan work (leaded glass field) from the industrial work (IGU sealing), and provides the clean sealing surface the IGU fabricator requires.

---

## The Triple-Glaze Connection

Standard US residential windows are dual-glazed, which would normally leave no protected cavity for a decorative middle panel. Two paths resolve this:

1. **Order unglazed frames** — the window dealer orders the frame without a sealed unit; the stained glass IGU is installed as the glazing. Works for new window orders.

2. **Replacement sealed units** — because `ProductLineKey` is already captured on `ConfiguredWindowItemEntity`, the platform already knows the exact rebate depth and spacer geometry for AWW EnergySaver, Apex, and Carriage frames. A triple-glazed replacement unit can be specced to drop into an existing frame the dealer previously sold. This makes the window dealer a repeat-purchase channel rather than a one-time transaction.

---

## The Marketplace Model

The natural distribution channel is **through window dealers**, not direct to homeowners:

- The dealer is already in the home, already measuring windows for replacement
- The frame measurement is already captured in the session
- The stained glass insert becomes an upsell line item in the same quote session
- The daylight opening is derivable from the frame measurement already on hand

The studio becomes a **fulfillment partner tenant** — a second tenant type that receives fabrication spec webhooks rather than CRM handoff webhooks. Same platform, different role, different payload.

**Supply chain:**
- Window dealer (sales tenant) → owns the customer relationship, captures measurements
- Stained glass studio (fulfillment tenant) → receives job spec, fabricates, delivers

---

## Why This Might Work

Stained glass inserts were mainstream in Victorian and Craftsman homes. What suppressed demand wasn't taste — it was cost, friction, and the disappearance of accessible price points in the postwar tract housing era. The configurator removes the primary friction: the design consultation and custom quoting process that made every job start from zero.

The pattern library approach — parametric designs that scale and tile to any dimension — means the artist stops being a designer-for-hire and becomes a fabricator working from a pre-validated spec. Skilled time goes into cutting, leading, and soldering. The platform handles everything upstream.

## Where We Started

Before today, the codebase had `OrderEntity` (customer name, address, hardcoded Salesforce IDs) and `OrderItemEntity` (literally just a Guid). Those were leftovers from the original NALP commerce integration. They implied order fulfillment semantics — inventory, payment, commitment — none of which belong in a pre-order configuration platform. Nobody had cleaned them up because the configurator UI worked around them and the real domain model hadn't been defined yet.

The right move was to define the real model first and let everything else follow from it.

---

## The Three Domain Entities

### Why TenantEntity first

Tenant had to come first because everything else belongs to a tenant. Without a tenant record, there's no place to hang API credentials, webhook callback URLs, product line access rules, or branding configuration. Building sessions or items without a tenant anchor would produce a model that's technically functional but architecturally rootless — you'd have to retrofit multi-tenancy later, which is expensive.

`TenantEntity` captures:
- `ApiKey` — how the CRM authenticates
- `WebhookCallbackUrl` — where completed quotes get POSTed
- `AllowedProductLineKeys` — which product lines the dealer is licensed for
- `MixedProductLinesAllowed` — a policy flag discussed in detail below
- `Branding` — logo and colors for white-label embedding

The branding is kept as a nested `TenantBrandingConfig` object rather than a JSON blob or separate table. Either of those would work fine when persistence comes in, and the object shape gives us strong typing now without committing to a storage strategy.

### Why QuoteSession next

`QuoteSessionEntity` replaces `OrderEntity`. The name change matters. An "order" implies something has been purchased. A "quote session" implies something is being explored and priced — which is what this system actually does. It's pre-order. The CRM handles post-submission.

The key design decisions in `QuoteSessionEntity`:

**`DefaultProductLineKey` is nullable and named "default," not "productLine."** This was deliberate. For CRM-initiated sessions, the CRM often knows the product line and passes it in at session creation. For website-initiated sessions with a tenant who allows only one product line, it gets set automatically. But for mixed-product-line quotes (see below), it's a hint, not a constraint. Naming it "default" makes that distinction explicit.

**`ExternalReferenceId`** carries the CRM's opportunity or record ID back out in the webhook payload. The CRM doesn't need to store our ID — it already has its own. This field lets the two systems correlate without either one needing to know the other's schema.

**`MagicLinkToken` and `MagicLinkExpiresAt`** exist only for website-initiated sessions. Their presence or absence implicitly identifies which flow created the session. A null magic link token = CRM-initiated. Non-null = prospect. This is simpler than a separate discriminator field.

**`ExpiresAt`** exists because sessions that get created and abandoned shouldn't live forever. A CRM that creates a session and never sends the contractor is a real scenario. Expiry gives us a clean state to transition to.

### Why ConfiguredWindowItem last

`ConfiguredWindowItemEntity` replaces `OrderItemEntity` (which was just a Guid). This is where the richest domain knowledge lives.

The most important structural decision was **storing the full configuration as a JSON blob (`ConfigurationJson`) alongside promoted scalar fields.** The alternative — fully normalizing every option (style, grille pattern, SDL settings, pane configuration, etc.) into relational columns — would be expensive to design, fragile to maintain as the configurator evolves, and unnecessary for the primary use cases. The CRM doesn't need to query "show me all windows with Ladder grille pattern." The server pricing engine and validation engine need to read the full configuration, which they can do from the JSON blob. What does need to be queryable is promoted: frame dimensions, rough opening dimensions, outside dimensions, product line, section count, price, status.

**The three measurement pairs** were a significant domain conversation today.

The system originally had variables called `osmWidth`/`osmHeight` in the JavaScript — "outside measure" — and I had initially described these as the manufacturing-critical dimension. That was corrected with important nuance:

1. **Frame measurement** — outside edge to outside edge of the inner frame including the jamb extension. This is the window unit size, what you order from the manufacturer.

2. **Rough opening** — the structural hole in the wall. The rule of thumb is frame + ½" on all sides, but that's an approximation. Before a purchase order is placed, a contractor does a **remeasure visit**: casing comes off, the actual opening is measured. The real rough opening can differ from the estimate. This means rough opening must be stored as a first-class field, not derived. The null fields (`RoughOpeningWidthDecimal`, `RoughOpeningHeightDecimal`) are meaningful state — null means the remeasure hasn't happened yet, which means the item is not order-ready.

3. **Outside measurement** — the dimension that must fit within the existing brick or vinyl surround. This is variable because it depends on whether the old window had brickmould, whether the new window has brickmould, and how the original installation was done. If the original install was brickmould-to-brick and the new window also has brickmould, the outside measurement is brickmould-to-brickmould. If you're fitting into a tight vinyl surround with no brickmould clearance, the outside measurement is the frame. These are genuinely different numbers and cannot be computed from the frame alone — they require the brickmould configuration as an input.

The workflow implication: rough opening null = quote in progress. Rough opening populated = contractor has remeasured, item is order-ready. This distinction will matter for the API validation layer in Phase 6.

## Mixed Product Lines

This came up as a nuanced domain question. The `MixedProductLinesAllowed` flag on `TenantEntity` was discussed in context of the difference between a quote and a purchase order.

At the **quote stage**, mixing product lines is entirely legitimate. A contractor is pricing a whole house renovation. Some windows might be Apex (mid-range), some might be EnergySaver (budget), depending on location, egress requirements, or customer preference. The quote is an estimate — flexibility makes sense.

At the **order stage**, mixing product lines is operationally impossible. Window manufacturers produce per product line. You cannot mix Apex and EnergySaver on one purchase order. A mixed-product-line quote, when converted to purchase, must become two separate orders.

This has a direct implication for the webhook payload: a completed quote with mixed product lines must group items by product line in the payload. The `orderGroups` structure in `QuoteCompletedPayload` was designed for exactly this. The CRM admin who receives it can create one purchase order per group. Most quotes will have one group — the structure costs nothing for the common case but handles the multi-product-line case correctly without a breaking change.

## Architecture Decision Records

We recognized today that significant architectural decisions had been made in the May 11 and May 13 sessions without being formally recorded. Journals are narrative and retrospective — they capture what happened, not what was decided and why. ADRs are structured for future reference: context, decision, alternatives rejected, consequences.

Five ADRs were written, four of them backfilled from the journals:

- **ADR 0001** — Unidirectional webhook model. We never call the CRM. The CRM expert on the tenant's side owns their integration. We fire a generic webhook; they interpret it. This keeps the platform CRM-agnostic and eliminates adapter maintenance forever.

- **ADR 0002** — Two-flow architecture. CRM-initiated (contractor with machine authentication) and website-initiated (prospect with magic link) share the same session model and webhook pattern. The flows differ in how they start, not in what they produce.

- **ADR 0003** — Session-centric model. "Order" was the wrong word for what this system does. The CRM owns the downstream sales lifecycle. We own pre-order configuration state. That boundary clarification drove the rename from `OrderEntity`/`OrderItemEntity` to `QuoteSession`/`ConfiguredWindowItem`.

- **ADR 0004** — Product line as session-level input. Product line is a project-level decision in real contractor workflows, usually provided by the CRM context. Treating it as a casual per-window UI choice was a carryover from the original commerce integration that didn't reflect reality.

- **ADR 0005** — Completion webhook payload contract. The most consequential for downstream consumers. Once a CRM admin builds against this payload, breaking it is expensive. The structure was designed to be stable: guaranteed fields are guaranteed, nullable fields are documented as nullable with explicit business reasons, and the order-group structure handles mixed product lines without requiring a v2 of the payload.

The lesson going forward: write ADRs the same day decisions get made, not backfilled from journals weeks later. The journals are still valuable — they're the narrative record — but they don't replace structured decision documentation.

## Test Scaffold

Testing came up in the context of "we should get some testing into the data layer starting now." The reasoning was sound: the configurator JS isn't unit-testable in its current form, and trying to retrofit a test project after the repository layer is built is harder than building it before. The scaffolding cost today is low. The payoff comes in Phase 2 (repository layer — can we save and load a session correctly?) and Phase 4 (pricing — does the server compute the right price for a given configuration?).

The 18 tests written today are deliberately thin — the entities are POCOs with no business logic. What the tests do cover:
- Default values are correct (Draft status, null pricing, null rough opening, null magic link)
- The two session flow shapes (CRM-initiated and website-initiated) produce the expected field presence/absence
- All three measurement pairs can be independently stored
- Items can be linked to sessions

More importantly, the test project is in the solution, the reference to `WindowConfigurator.Data` is wired up, and the CI baseline is established. Every future phase has somewhere to put its tests immediately.
